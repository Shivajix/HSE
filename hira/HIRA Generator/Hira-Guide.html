Learn how to effectively identify hazards and assess risks

What is HIRA?

In every industry or construction project, risks exist at every step. To manage them effectively, organizations rely on Hazard Identification & Risk Assessment (HIRA) — a systematic process to identify hazards, assess their severity and likelihood, and implement suitable controls.

Digital HIRA Advantage

By using a digital HIRA Generator, you can streamline this process, auto-calculate risk scores, and export professional PDF reports for record keeping.

HIRA Process Explained

HIRA is a structured process where each task/step is analyzed for potential hazards, and the risk level is determined using:

  • Severity (S) — consequence of hazard if it occurs (1–5)
  • Likelihood (L) — probability of occurrence (1–5)
  • Risk Rating (R) = S × L
  • Risk Level — Classified as Low, Medium, High, or Extreme

This ensures hazards are not overlooked and proper controls are planned.

Key Components of a HIRA

A robust HIRA document typically includes:

  • Project Meta Information — Site, activity, location, date, prepared/reviewed/approved by
  • Risk Matrix — Severity and likelihood scoring system
  • Hazard Identification — Potential hazards like fall, fire, electrical shock, etc.
  • Existing Controls — PPE, engineering controls, administrative controls
  • Additional Controls — Further measures required (training, equipment, supervision)
  • Risk Level Classification — Low, Medium, High, Extreme
  • Summary & Sign-off — Final approval with signatures

How to Use the HIRA Generator

  1. Enter Project Meta — Fill site, activity, location, date, and responsible persons
  2. Add Tasks/Steps — Each item represents a specific activity (e.g., 'Scaffolding erection')
  3. Identify Hazards — List the potential hazards for each task
  4. Select Severity & Likelihood — System calculates risk rating automatically
  5. Add Additional Controls — E.g., 'Job safety analysis', 'Toolbox talk'
  6. Enter Residual Risk — The system calculates residual risk after controls
  7. Check Summary — Overview of all risks with color-coded risk levels
  8. Download PDF Report — Professional report with risk matrix and signatures

If the risk remains high even after controls, apply additional measures — only proceed if residual risk falls within acceptable range.

Example Usage

Example 1 - Working at Height:
  • Hazard: Fall from height
  • Initial Risk: Severity 5 × Likelihood 4 = 20 (High)
  • Controls: Fall protection, safety harness, supervision
  • Residual Risk: Severity 3 × Likelihood 2 = 6 (Medium)
Example 2 - Working in Confined Space:
  • Hazards: Fire, toxic atmosphere, oxygen deficiency
  • Initial Risk: Severity 5 × Likelihood 3 = 15 (High)
  • Controls: Gas testing, permit to work, ventilation, fire watch
  • Residual Risk: Severity 2 × Likelihood 2 = 4 (Low)

Common Mistakes in HIRA

  • X Copying hazards without site-specific adaptations
  • X Relying only on PPE instead of eliminating hazards at source
  • X Underestimating likelihood (e.g., assuming rare = impossible)
  • X Skipping residual risk assessment after controls

Best Practices for Effective HIRA

  • ✓ Use a standard 5×5 risk matrix (adjust if your policy differs)
  • ✓ Involve workers in hazard identification
  • ✓ Review HIRA before every high-risk activity or when conditions change
  • ✓ Ensure residual risks are at acceptable level before proceeding
  • ✓ Integrate HIRA with JSA, PTW, and Incident Tracking Systems

Frequently Asked Questions

How often should HIRA be reviewed? +

HIRA should be reviewed before each high-risk activity or whenever conditions change significantly (new equipment, personnel, or work environment).

Can HIRA replace JSA (Job Safety Analysis)? +

No. While both tools focus on safety, HIRA is broader risk classification at activity level, while JSA provides step-by-step analysis of specific tasks.

Who should be involved in HIRA preparation? +

HIRA should involve supervisors, safety officers, and most importantly, workers who perform the tasks as they have practical knowledge of hazards.
