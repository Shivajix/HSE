Key Incident Metrics Explained

Incident Rate (IR)

IR = (Number of Recordable Incidents × 200,000) / Employee Hours Worked

Incident Rate measures the number of recordable incidents per 100 full-time employees during a one-year period. It's the most commonly used safety metric for overall safety performance.

DART Rate

DART = (Days Away, Restricted or Transferred × 200,000) / Employee Hours Worked

DART Rate measures cases involving days away from work, restricted work activity, or job transfer per 100 full-time employees. It focuses on more serious incidents.

LWDI Rate

LWDI = (Lost Workday Cases × 200,000) / Employee Hours Worked

Lost Workday Injury (LWDI) Rate measures cases involving days away from work per 100 full-time employees. It specifically tracks the most severe incidents.

Types of Recordable Incidents

Medical Treatment Cases

Incidents requiring medical treatment beyond first aid, including stitches, prescription medication, or physical therapy.

Lost Workday Cases

Incidents resulting in days away from work following the day of injury, indicating more serious injuries.

Restricted Work Cases

Incidents where employees can work but with restrictions on duties or hours due to work-related injury or illness.

Other Recordables

Other OSHA recordable cases including needlesticks, hearing loss, cancer, and chronic irreversible diseases.

How to Calculate Incident Rate

1

Identify Recordable Incidents

Gather data on all recordable incidents that occurred during the measurement period. According to OSHA, recordable incidents include fatalities, lost workday cases, restricted work cases, medical treatment beyond first aid, and other specific cases.

2

Calculate Total Employee Hours

Calculate the total number of hours worked by all employees during the same measurement period. Include all hours worked - regular time, overtime, but exclude vacation, sick leave, holidays, and other non-work hours.

3

Apply the Incident Rate Formula

Use the formula: IR = (Number of Recordable Incidents × 200,000) / Total Employee Hours Worked. The 200,000 factor represents 100 employees working 40 hours per week for 50 weeks per year, standardizing the rate.

4

Interpret and Compare Results

Compare your calculated IR with previous periods, organizational targets, and industry benchmarks. Lower IR values indicate better safety performance and fewer workplace incidents.
