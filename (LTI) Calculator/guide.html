What Is Lost Time Injury (LTI)?

A Lost Time Injury (LTI) is a workplace injury that results in an employee being unable to perform their regular job duties for at least one full day beyond the day of the injury. This is one of the most important safety metrics as it indicates serious incidents with significant impact on workers and operations.

LTI Definition: Any work-related injury or illness that results in an employee being unable to work their next scheduled shift or being placed on restricted/light duty for one or more days.

LTIs differ from other recordable incidents because they specifically track injuries that result in time away from regular work duties, making them a key indicator of serious safety issues.

Formula for Total LTI

There are several key formulas used to calculate and analyze Lost Time Injuries, each providing different insights into safety performance:

Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate (LTIFR)

The most common metric for comparing LTI performance across organizations and industries.

LTIFR = (Number of LTIs × 1,000,000) / Total Hours Worked

This formula standardizes the rate to 1,000,000 hours worked, allowing comparison between organizations of different sizes.

Lost Time Injury Severity Rate (LTISR)

Measures the impact of LTIs in terms of total days lost from work.

LTISR = (Total Lost Days × 1,000,000) / Total Hours Worked

This helps organizations understand not just how often injuries occur, but their severity and impact on operations.

OSHA Lost Workday Injury Rate

The standard measurement used in the United States for regulatory reporting.

LTI Rate = (Number of LTIs × 200,000) / Total Hours Worked

This uses 200,000 hours as the base (equivalent to 100 employees working full-time for one year).

Example Calculations

Here are practical examples of how LTI metrics are calculated in different workplace scenarios:

Construction Company Example

Scenario: A construction firm with 150 employees working 500,000 total hours experiences 4 LTIs with a total of 62 lost days.

LTIFR Calculation: (4 × 1,000,000) / 500,000 = 8.0

LTISR Calculation: (62 × 1,000,000) / 500,000 = 124.0

Interpretation: The company has a LTIFR of 8.0, which is slightly better than the construction industry average of 8.5.

Manufacturing Plant Example

Scenario: A manufacturing facility with 300 employees working 600,000 total hours experiences 3 LTIs with a total of 45 lost days.

LTIFR Calculation: (3 × 1,000,000) / 600,000 = 5.0

LTISR Calculation: (45 × 1,000,000) / 600,000 = 75.0

OSHA Rate: (3 × 200,000) / 600,000 = 1.0

Interpretation: The facility performs significantly better than the manufacturing industry average LTIFR of 6.2.

Healthcare Facility Example

Scenario: A hospital with 500 employees working 1,000,000 total hours experiences 7 LTIs with a total of 105 lost days.

LTIFR Calculation: (7 × 1,000,000) / 1,000,000 = 7.0

LTISR Calculation: (105 × 1,000,000) / 1,000,000 = 105.0

Interpretation: The hospital's LTIFR of 7.0 is slightly higher than the healthcare industry average of 6.5, indicating room for improvement.

Why Is Tracking LTI Important?

Tracking Lost Time Injuries is critical for several important reasons that impact both safety outcomes and business performance:

Serious Incident Indicator

LTIs represent the most serious workplace injuries with significant human and operational impact.

Regulatory Compliance

Required by OSHA and other regulatory bodies for reporting and compliance purposes.

Performance Benchmarking

Allows comparison with industry averages and helps set realistic safety improvement targets.

Cost Management

LTIs are associated with high direct and indirect costs including workers' compensation and productivity losses.

Prevention Focus

Helps identify high-risk activities and prioritize preventive measures.

Stakeholder Confidence

Demonstrates commitment to safety to employees, clients, investors, and regulators.

Did You Know? The average Lost Time Injury costs organizations between $38,000 and $150,000 in direct and indirect costs, including medical expenses, lost productivity, and administrative costs.

Real-World Examples

Understanding how LTI tracking applies in real-world scenarios helps contextualize the metrics and their importance:

A large construction company reduced its LTIFR from 12.5 to 4.2 over three years by implementing a comprehensive safety program focused on fall protection, equipment safety, and supervisor training. A manufacturing facility identified through LTI analysis that musculoskeletal disorders accounted for 65% of their lost time injuries. They implemented an ergonomics program with workstation redesign, employee training, and early intervention that reduced LTIs by 58% in two years.

Case Study: Warehouse Slip and Fall Reduction

A distribution center used LTI data to identify that slips, trips, and falls were their leading cause of lost time injuries. By implementing improved flooring, better lighting, and housekeeping protocols, they reduced these incidents by 72% and decreased their LTIFR from 8.3 to 3.1.

Common Mistakes in Tracking LTIs

Avoid these common errors when tracking and analyzing Lost Time Injuries:

Inconsistent Classification

Applying different criteria for what constitutes a Lost Time Injury across departments or locations.

Critical

Underreporting

Failing to record all LTIs due to pressure to maintain low injury rates or lack of reporting culture.

Critical

Inaccurate Hours Data

Using estimated rather than actual hours worked, especially for contractors and temporary workers.

Important

Focusing Only on Frequency

Ignoring severity rates and the underlying causes of the most serious injuries.

Important

Lack of Root Cause Analysis

Tracking LTIs without investigating and addressing the underlying causes.

Critical

Inappropriate Benchmarking

Comparing LTI rates with organizations in different industries without proper context.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What's the difference between LTI and recordable incident?

A recordable incident is any work-related injury or illness that requires medical treatment beyond first aid, while an LTI specifically refers to incidents that result in time away from regular work duties.

How many days away from work defines an LTI?

An LTI is typically defined as any injury that results in an employee being unable to work their next regularly scheduled work day or being placed on restricted duty.

Should restricted work cases be included in LTI counts?

Yes, both days away from work and restricted work cases are generally included in LTI calculations, though some organizations track them separately for more detailed analysis.

What is a good LTIFR for my industry?

Good LTIFR values vary by industry. Construction typically averages 8-10, manufacturing 5-7, and office environments 1-2. Always compare with current industry benchmarks.

How often should we calculate our LTI rates?

Most organizations calculate LTI rates quarterly for trend analysis and annually for benchmarking and reporting purposes.

Can we have a zero LTI rate?

While challenging, a zero LTI rate is possible with exceptional safety programs. However, focusing on continuous improvement is often more realistic than pursuing absolute zero.
