What Is Severity Rate (SR)?

Severity Rate (SR) is a critical safety performance indicator that measures the severity of workplace injuries by calculating the average number of days lost or charged per recordable injury. Unlike Frequency Rate which measures how often injuries occur, Severity Rate focuses on the impact and consequences of those injuries.

Definition: Severity Rate represents the total number of days lost or charged due to workplace injuries per 1,000,000 hours worked. It provides insight into the seriousness of injuries and their impact on workforce productivity and well-being.

Severity Rate is particularly valuable because it:

Measures Impact

Quantifies the actual impact of injuries in terms of lost workdays and productivity

Identifies Serious Risks

Highlights high-consequence incidents that may require different prevention strategies

Supports Resource Allocation

Helps prioritize safety investments based on the severity of potential outcomes

Key Characteristics
  • Focus on Impact: Measures consequences rather than frequency
  • Standardized Unit: Days lost per 1,000,000 hours worked
  • Complementary Metric: Used alongside Frequency Rate for complete safety picture
  • Business Impact: Directly relates to productivity loss and workers' compensation costs

Formula for Severity Rate (SR)

The Severity Rate formula calculates the average number of days lost per recordable injury, standardized to 1,000,000 hours worked:

Standard Severity Rate Formula

SR = (Total Days Lost × 1,000,000) ÷ Total Hours Worked

This formula provides a standardized measure that allows comparison across organizations of different sizes.

Understanding "Days Lost": Days lost include actual days away from work plus days of restricted work activity or job transfer. Some organizations also include permanent impairments and fatalities with standardized day charges (typically 6,000 days for a fatality).

Days Lost Calculation

Total Days Lost = Days Away From Work + Days of Restricted Work + Standardized Charges

Where standardized charges might include:

  • Fatality: 6,000 days
  • Permanent total disability: 6,000 days
  • Permanent partial disability: Varies by impairment rating

Component Description Example
Days Away From Work Calendar days employee could not work due to injury 14 days
Restricted Work Days Days employee worked with restrictions due to injury 7 days
Standard Charges Pre-set values for serious outcomes (fatalities, disabilities) 6,000 days (fatality)

  6. Take Action: Implement targeted interventions for high-severity risks

Scenario: A construction company with 85 total days lost (65 days away + 20 restricted) over 450,000 hours worked

Calculation: (85 × 1,000,000) ÷ 450,000 = 188.89 SR

Calculation: ((6,000 + 40) × 1,000,000) ÷ 600,000 = 10,066.67 SR

Interpretation: This extremely high SR reflects a serious incident with fatal outcome

Analysis: The fatality dramatically increases SR, highlighting need for serious injury prevention

Calculation: (120 × 1,000,000) ÷ 280,000 = 428.57 SR

Interpretation: This SR is typical for oil & gas industry (avg: 300-500)

Analysis: Moderate SR suggests mix of minor and more serious injuries

Highlights high-consequence incidents that may require different prevention strategies than minor injuries.

Resource Prioritization

Helps prioritize safety investments toward preventing the most serious types of injuries.

Enables comparison with industry averages and identification of improvement opportunities.

Common Mistakes in Calculating Severity Rate

Avoid these common pitfalls to ensure accurate and meaningful Severity Rate calculations:

Inconsistent Day Counting

Using different methods to count lost days (calendar days vs. work days) across incidents or over time.

Fix: Standardize on calendar days for all lost time calculations and document the methodology.

Critical

Ignoring Restricted Work

Failing to include days of restricted work or job transfer in total days lost calculations.

Fix: Ensure all forms of lost productivity (away, restricted, transfer) are captured consistently.

Critical

Small Sample Size Interpretation

Drawing significant conclusions from SR calculated with very few incidents or hours.

Fix: Use larger time periods (annual or multi-year) for meaningful SR analysis and trend identification.

Important

Excluding Contractor Data

Not including contractor injuries and hours in SR calculations for sites where contractors work.

Fix: Include all workers (employees and contractors) in SR calculations for complete picture.

Important

Misunderstanding SR Volatility

Overreacting to SR fluctuations caused by single serious incidents in small populations.

Fix: Understand that SR can be volatile; focus on long-term trends rather than short-term changes.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) on Severity Rate Calculator

What's the difference between Severity Rate and Frequency Rate?

Frequency Rate measures how often injuries occur (number of injuries per hours worked), while Severity Rate measures the impact of those injuries (days lost per hours worked). FR tells you about injury frequency, SR tells you about injury consequences.

How are fatalities counted in Severity Rate calculations?

Most organizations use a standardized charge for fatalities, typically 6,000 days. This represents the permanent loss and allows meaningful comparison across organizations. Some standards may use different values, so consistency within your organization is key.

Should we include light duty or restricted work in Severity Rate?

Yes, days of restricted work or job transfer should be included in Severity Rate calculations. These represent lost productivity and are an important component of understanding the full impact of injuries.

What is a good Severity Rate?

A lower SR is always better. While industry averages vary, many organizations aim for SR below 100. World-class safety performers often achieve rates below 50. The ultimate goal should be zero injuries, reflected in an SR of 0.0.

How can a single serious incident affect our Severity Rate?

A single serious incident (like a fatality or permanent disability) can dramatically increase Severity Rate, especially in smaller organizations. This is why SR should be interpreted in context and tracked over longer periods to identify true trends.

Should we track Severity Rate if we have zero injuries?

Yes, even with zero injuries, tracking Severity Rate (which would be 0.0) provides important baseline data and demonstrates excellent safety performance. It also prepares your organization for consistent metric tracking when injuries do occur.

Final Thoughts on Severity Rate Calculator

Severity Rate is a powerful metric that complements Frequency Rate by focusing on the consequences rather than just the frequency of workplace injuries. While a low Frequency Rate indicates fewer injuries occur, a low Severity Rate indicates that when injuries do happen, their impact is minimized.

The true value of Severity Rate lies in its ability to highlight serious injury risks that might be masked by focusing solely on injury frequency. Organizations that effectively manage both Frequency Rate and Severity Rate typically experience not only better safety performance but also significant operational and financial benefits.
