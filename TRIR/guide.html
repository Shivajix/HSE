What Are TRIR, AFR, and FAR?

TRIR, AFR, and FAR are key safety metrics used to measure workplace safety performance across industries.

TRIR Definition: Total Recordable Incident Rate measures the number of recordable incidents per 100 full-time employees during a one-year period.

Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR)

A comprehensive metric that includes fatalities, lost workday cases, restricted work cases, and medical treatment cases (excluding first aid).

All Injury Frequency Rate (AFR)

Measures the frequency of all workplace injuries, including those requiring medical treatment beyond first aid.

Fatal Accident Rate (FAR)

Calculates the number of fatalities per 100 million hours worked, providing perspective on the most serious safety outcomes.

Formulas

Each safety metric has a specific formula for calculation:

TRIR Formula

TRIR = (Number of Recordable Incidents × 200,000) / Total Hours Worked

Where 200,000 represents 100 employees working 40 hours per week for 50 weeks.

AFR Formula

AFR = (Number of Injuries × 1,000,000) / Total Hours Worked

Where 1,000,000 represents the base for 500 employees.

FAR Formula

FAR = (Number of Fatalities × 100,000,000) / Total Hours Worked

Where 100,000,000 represents 1,000 employees working over 50 years.

Note: These formulas allow for standardized comparison of safety performance across organizations of different sizes and industries.

Example Calculations for TRIR Calculator

Here are practical examples of how to calculate TRIR, AFR, and FAR:

Manufacturing Company Example

Given: 5 recordable incidents, 500,000 hours worked, 2 fatalities, 8 injuries

TRIR Calculation: (5 × 200,000) / 500,000 = 2.0

AFR Calculation: (8 × 1,000,000) / 500,000 = 16.0

FAR Calculation: (2 × 100,000,000) / 500,000 = 400.0

Construction Company Example

Given: 12 recordable incidents, 750,000 hours worked, 1 fatality, 15 injuries

TRIR Calculation: (12 × 200,000) / 750,000 = 3.2

AFR Calculation: (15 × 1,000,000) / 750,000 = 20.0

FAR Calculation: (1 × 100,000,000) / 750,000 = 133.3

Healthcare Facility Example

Given: 8 recordable incidents, 1,200,000 hours worked, 0 fatalities, 10 injuries

TRIR Calculation: (8 × 200,000) / 1,200,000 = 1.33

AFR Calculation: (10 × 1,000,000) / 1,200,000 = 8.33

FAR Calculation: (0 × 100,000,000) / 1,200,000 = 0.0

Why Are TRIR, AFR, and FAR Important?

Tracking TRIR, AFR, and FAR provides critical insights into workplace safety performance and organizational risk management.

Regulatory Compliance

Many regulatory bodies require tracking and reporting of safety metrics for compliance purposes.

Benchmarking

Allows comparison of safety performance against industry averages and competitors.

Risk Assessment

Helps identify high-risk areas and prioritize safety interventions.

Performance Measurement

Quantifies the effectiveness of safety programs and initiatives.

Insurance and Liability

Impacts insurance premiums and can influence legal liability in case of incidents.

Did You Know? Organizations with lower TRIR rates typically experience higher productivity, lower turnover, and better overall business performance.

Common Mistakes in Tracking

Avoid these common pitfalls to ensure accurate and meaningful safety metric tracking:

Applying different criteria for what constitutes a recordable incident across departments or over time.

Critical

Underreporting

Failing to record all incidents due to fear of consequences or lack of reporting culture.

Inaccurate Hours Tracking

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What is considered a recordable incident?

A recordable incident is any work-related injury or illness that results in death, loss of consciousness, days away from work, restricted work activity, job transfer, or medical treatment beyond first aid.

How often should TRIR be calculated?

TRIR is typically calculated annually, but many organizations track it monthly or quarterly to monitor trends and implement timely interventions.

What is a good TRIR rate?

A lower TRIR is always better. While industry averages vary, many organizations aim for TRIR below 2.0, with world-class safety performers achieving rates below 1.0.

Should contractor hours be included in TRIR calculations?

Yes, contractor hours should be included if the organization is responsible for tracking and reporting safety performance for all workers at the site.

Can TRIR be zero?

Yes, many organizations achieve zero recordable incidents, resulting in a TRIR of 0.0. This represents excellent safety performance.

How do I improve our TRIR?

Focus on proactive safety measures: hazard identification and control, employee training, safety leadership, incident investigation, and continuous improvement of safety systems.
