Companies want to know not just how productive their workforce is, but also how safely that work is being done. One of the most widely used measures is Safe Man Hours—the total number of hours worked without any Lost Time Injury (LTI).

What Are Safe Man Hours?

Safe Man Hours represent the number of working hours completed by employees without any incident that results in lost time.

If no LTIs occur

Hours are added to the cumulative safe man hours.

If an LTI occurs

Safe hours reset back to zero, and counting begins again.

Example

If 100 workers each work 8 hours a day without any lost time injuries, the site achieves 800 safe man hours for that day.

Safe man hours are often announced in toolbox talks, safety meetings, project boards, and client reports as a way of celebrating progress and reinforcing safety culture.

Formula for Safe Man Hours

Safe Man Hours = Number of Employees × Hours Worked per Employee

If there are no LTIs, this value is added to the previous safe hours.

If there is an LTI, the safe hours reset to zero.

Why Are Safe Man Hours Important?

Tracking safe man hours is more than just a calculation—it reflects the safety culture of an organization.

Performance Indicator

Shows how long a site has worked without injury.

Employee Motivation

Workers are proud to achieve milestones like "1 Million Safe Hours".

Client & Regulatory Reporting

Demonstrates compliance and safety excellence in tenders, audits, and reports.

Benchmarking

Helps companies compare safety performance across sites or projects.

Real-World Examples

Construction Site Example

200 workers on a metro project working 10 hours/day for 30 days.

Total man-hours = 200 x 10 x 30 = 60,000 Safe Man Hours (if no LTI occurs).

Manufacturing Plant Example

150 workers on 8-hour shifts for 25 days.

Total man-hours = 150 x 8 x 25 = 30,000 Safe Man Hours.

Oil & Gas Industry Example

500 workers x 12 hours/day x 365 days = 2,190,000 Safe Man Hours in a year (if no LTI).

Common Mistakes in Tracking Safe Hours

  • Forgetting to include contractors' hours.
  • Misreporting overtime hours.
  • Not resetting after an LTI.
  • Rounding off hours without accurate records.

Always keep proper attendance, timesheets, and incident logs to ensure accuracy.

Best Practices for Achieving High Safe Man Hours

  • Conduct daily toolbox talks.
  • Enforce Permit-to-Work (PTW) systems.
  • Carry out regular safety inspections.
  • Ensure proper training & inductions for new employees.
  • Promote a "Stop Work Authority" culture where anyone can halt unsafe work.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

What happens to safe man hours after a minor first-aid case? +

First-aid cases do not affect safe man hours because they do not cause lost time.

Are safe man hours the same as total man-hours? +

No. Safe man hours exclude any period when an LTI occurs.

Can safe man hours be used in tenders? +

Yes—companies often showcase safe man hours in bids, client reports and milestone boards.

Does an LTI reset the counter? +

Yes. After an LTI, safe hours reset to zero and counting restarts from the next working day.

Should overtime and contractor hours be included? +

Yes. Include actual hours worked by employees and in-scope contractors, including overtime, for accuracy.
